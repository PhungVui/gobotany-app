Getting Started

What kinds of plants can I identify?

The Go Botany Plant Identifier helps you identify thousands of native and naturalized plant species in New England. You can also identify subspecies and varieties. Many of these plants grow outside of New England, though, so this key is useful well beyond our region.

What should I notice about my plant?

  • Choose a plant that you can look at closely enough to answer some questions about its characteristics. More...
  • You may want to collect all or part of the plant, or take some photos or make sketches that you can refer to later.
  • Note the plant's location and the habitat it is growing in.

How do I get started using the Plant Identifier?

Click to enlarge
  • Choose the plant group you think your plant is in. You'll be shown some choices. Compare the features of your plant with the "Key Characteristics" listed for each plant group and "Exceptions," which will help you avoid mixing up groups. More...
  • If your plant isn't in one of the first three groups, press the "Show me other groups" button and new choices will appear.
  • When you've found the group your plant belongs to, press "My plant is in this group."

What's a plant group?

Click to enlarge
  • It's a set of plants that share some common traits that would cause many people to think of them as similar. For example, the Aquatic Plant group contains many species of plants that live in or near water. More...
  • We have defined our groups as handy, contrasting categories that are easy to tell apart. They are not strict scientific classifications.
  • A few plant species belong to more than one group in our system, but most have been assigned to a single group with the characteristics that match them best.

Once I select a group, what happens next?

Click to enlarge
  • Next, choose which subgroup your plant belongs to. For example, if your plant is woody like a tree, you'll be asked if it is a needle-leaved plant or a broad-leaved plant.
  • When you've found the subgroup your plant belongs to, click "My plant is in this subgroup." More...
  • If your plant doesn't fit any of the subgroups, click the "Go back one level" button to choose a new group.
  • Once you're familiar with the groups and subgroups, use the Plant Map on the left side of the page to jump directly to the subgroup your plant is in.

I've chosen my plant subgroup. What's next?

Click to enlarge
  • On the left side of the new page is a list of questions about your plant. Click on any question, and a working area will open in the center of the page, showing the full question, some helpful hints, and a set of illustrated choices for the answer. Click on the answer (i.e., the characteristic) that most closely matches your plant.
  • If you can only observe part of a plant, say the leaves, you can click on that feature in the section "What type of features can you see on your plant?" and you'll only be offered questions about leaves.
  • Click the "Get more choices" button and new questions will appear, sorted in order of their helpfulness in narrowing down your species.
  • View a set of thumbnail photographs of all the possible plants that match your description, listed in alphabetical order by scientific name.
  • Click on a photo to get a pop-up box with more information about a plant. If you decide that this is your plant, click on "That's My Plant" in the pop-up box and you'll be taken to the species full information page. More...
  • The set of thumbnails gets smaller as you answer more questions and hone in on your species by the process of elimination.
  • To delete questions from your list, click the X button next to the question.
